### Leadership & Team Management

**Q: "Tell me about your experience leading and developing teams."**

A: "I've led teams across multiple organizations with a focus on development and performance. At My-Credit, I trained staff on underwriting standards and client evaluation procedures, improving team capability and service quality. At Schmsoft Ventures, I provided ongoing coaching on customer service excellence and product knowledge. My leadership philosophy is based on three principles: First, clear expectations—everyone knows their goals and how success is measured. Second, continuous development—regular coaching, training, and feedback to build capability. Third, empowerment—giving team members ownership and supporting them to succeed. At Maendeleo Ya Wanawake, I facilitated 50+ workshops, which taught me how to engage diverse groups and create learning environments. I believe great leaders develop people, not just manage tasks."

**Q: "How would you handle an underperforming team member?"**

A: "I'd start with diagnosis—is it a skills gap, motivation issue, or unclear expectations? I'd have a private conversation to understand their perspective and any challenges they're facing. Then I'd create a performance improvement plan with specific, measurable goals and a timeline. I'd provide additional training or coaching as needed and schedule regular check-ins to monitor progress and provide support. Throughout, I'd be clear about expectations while being supportive and solution-oriented. If performance doesn't improve despite support, I'd escalate appropriately. However, I've found that most performance issues can be resolved through clear communication, proper training, and accountability."

**Q: "Describe your approach to staff scheduling and duty assignment."**

A: "I take a strategic approach to scheduling that balances business needs with staff development. I consider peak customer traffic times, individual strengths, and development opportunities. For example, I'd ensure experienced staff are available during busy periods while pairing newer team members with mentors for learning. I'd also rotate responsibilities so everyone develops diverse skills—today's sales floor associate could be tomorrow's design consultant. I believe in transparent scheduling with advance notice so staff can plan their lives, and I'd maintain flexibility for genuine emergencies. I'd also track performance data to optimize scheduling—if certain team combinations drive better results, I'd leverage that insight."

**Q: "How do you foster a culture of energy, integrity, and customer centricity?"**

A: "Culture starts with leadership modeling the behaviors you want to see. I demonstrate energy through enthusiasm, positive attitude, and proactive problem-solving. I show integrity by being transparent, keeping commitments, and holding myself to the same standards I expect from the team. I model customer centricity by treating every customer interaction as an opportunity to create value. Beyond modeling, I reinforce culture through recognition—celebrating team members who exemplify our values. I also create forums for team input and feedback, making everyone feel ownership of our success. Finally, I connect daily work to larger purpose—helping customers create beautiful homes is meaningful work, and I'd ensure the team feels that impact."

### Sales & Customer Service

**Q: "How would you inspire the sales team to achieve targets?"**

A: "I'd use a multi-faceted approach. First, clear goals—breaking monthly targets into weekly and daily milestones so progress is visible and achievable. Second, enablement—ensuring the team has product knowledge, sales skills, and tools to succeed. Third, recognition—celebrating wins publicly and acknowledging effort, not just results. Fourth, coaching—working alongside the team, demonstrating techniques, and providing real-time feedback. Fifth, removing obstacles—if something is preventing sales, I'd address it quickly. At Ngao Credit, I exceeded targets by maintaining a robust pipeline and coaching team members on consultative selling. I'd bring that same systematic, supportive approach to driving furniture sales."

**Q: "Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult customer complaint."**

A: "At My-Credit, a client was upset about a loan processing delay that wasn't our fault—it was a documentation issue from a third party. The client was frustrated and threatening to leave. I listened without interrupting, acknowledged their frustration, and apologized for the inconvenience even though it wasn't our error. Then I took ownership—I personally followed up with the third party, expedited the documentation, and kept the client informed daily. I also offered a small gesture of goodwill. The client not only stayed but became one of our strongest advocates, referring multiple other clients. The lesson: customers don't always care whose fault it is—they want someone to own the solution. That's the approach I'd bring to handling customer complaints at Kaziweza."

**Q: "How would you ensure excellent customer service across the team?"**

A: "I'd establish clear service standards—greeting customers within 30 seconds, active listening to understand needs, product knowledge to make recommendations, follow-up on orders, and handling complaints professionally. I'd train the team on these standards and model them myself. I'd also implement a feedback system—customer surveys, mystery shopping, or direct feedback—to measure service quality. I'd recognize team members who deliver exceptional service and coach those who need improvement. Finally, I'd create a customer-centric mindset by sharing customer success stories and connecting our work to the impact we have on customers' lives. Service excellence isn't a program—it's a daily commitment."

### Operations & Inventory Management

**Q: "What's your experience with inventory management and stock control?"**

A: "While I haven't managed physical inventory in retail, I have strong experience with systematic tracking, reconciliation, and accuracy. At My-Credit, I processed loan applications ensuring complete documentation—similar discipline to inventory documentation. At Tenakata, I maintained 100% compliance with reporting requirements, which required meticulous record-keeping. I understand the principles of inventory management: accurate counts, regular reconciliations, investigating discrepancies, coordinating replenishment, and preventing shrinkage. I'm a quick learner with systems, and I'm confident I can master your inventory procedures and ERP system rapidly. I'd also leverage my analytical skills to identify patterns—fast-moving items, seasonal trends, and optimization opportunities."

**Q: "How would you handle stock discrepancies?"**

A: "I'd approach it systematically. First, verify the discrepancy—recount the physical stock and check system records to confirm the gap. Second, investigate the cause—was it a counting error, documentation mistake, theft, damage, or system issue? Third, document everything—what was found, probable cause, and corrective action. Fourth, implement controls to prevent recurrence—better counting procedures, security measures, or system checks. Fifth, report appropriately to management. Throughout, I'd maintain objectivity and focus on solutions, not blame. The goal is accurate inventory and continuous improvement of processes."

**Q: "You don't have experience with Odoo or ERP systems. How will you learn?"**

A: "I have a strong track record of quickly mastering new systems. At Kwendo, I learned their digital platform rapidly and trained clients on it. At Ngao Credit and My-Credit, I used CRM and loan management systems effectively. I'm comfortable with technology and understand that ERP systems are tools to support business processes. I'd approach learning Odoo systematically: formal training if available, hands-on practice, learning from experienced users, and leveraging online resources. I'd focus first on the core functions I need daily—inventory tracking, sales processing, reporting—then expand to advanced features. I'm confident I can become proficient within 4-6 weeks and an expert within 3 months."

### Scenario-Based Questions

**Q: "It's month-end and you're behind on sales targets. What do you do?"**

A: "I'd take immediate action with a multi-pronged approach. First, analyze the gap—which product categories are underperforming and why? Second, rally the team—communicate the situation transparently, break the remaining target into daily goals, and create urgency without panic. Third, tactical actions—increase customer outreach, leverage our database for follow-ups, run a promotion if appropriate, ensure optimal showroom presentation, and extend operating hours if needed. Fourth, lead from the front—I'd be on the sales floor actively engaging customers and demonstrating the effort I expect. Fifth, remove obstacles—if something is preventing sales, I'd address it immediately. Finally, learn from it—what caused us to fall behind, and how do we prevent it next month?"

**Q: "You discover a team member has been stealing from the company. How do you handle it?"**

A: "This is a serious matter requiring immediate action. First, I'd gather facts—verify the theft through evidence, not assumptions. Second, I'd report to management immediately and follow company policy—this isn't something I'd handle alone. Third, I'd secure any evidence and prevent further loss. Fourth, I'd maintain confidentiality to protect both the investigation and the accused's rights until facts are established. Fifth, I'd cooperate fully with any investigation. Throughout, I'd be objective and follow proper procedures. If theft is confirmed, I'd also review our controls to understand how it happened and implement measures to prevent recurrence. Security and integrity are non-negotiable."

**Q: "A key team member wants to leave. How do you retain them?"**

A: "I'd start with a genuine conversation to understand why they want to leave—is it compensation, growth opportunities, work environment, or personal reasons? If it's something within my control, I'd work to address it—perhaps additional responsibilities, training opportunities, or schedule flexibility. I'd also highlight the value they bring and the opportunities ahead at Kaziweza. However, I'd be realistic—if they've made up their mind or the issue is beyond what we can address, I'd respect their decision and ensure a smooth transition. I'd also use it as a learning opportunity—exit interviews provide valuable insights for improving retention. The best retention strategy is creating an environment where people want to stay, which I'd focus on proactively."
