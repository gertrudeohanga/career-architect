## Critical Questions (Certification Gap)

### Q1: "I notice you don't have a Certificate of Proficiency in Insurance, which is a mandatory requirement. Can you explain?"

**Why they ask**: This is the elephant in the room. They need to understand if you're aware of the requirement and have a plan.

**Your response strategy**: Be direct, acknowledge it, show commitment, emphasize transferable skills

**Model Answer**:

"Thank you for raising that directly. You're absolutely right that the COP is a mandatory requirement, and I want to be transparent about my situation. I'm currently enrolled in the COP certification program and am committed to completing it within [X months/timeframe]. 

What I bring immediately is 5+ years of proven financial services sales success with a track record that directly translates to bancassurance: I've consistently exceeded sales targets by 20-35%, maintained 60-70% customer retention rates, and successfully sold complex financial products that require the same skills as insurance—understanding customer needs, explaining product benefits, ensuring documentation accuracy, and building trust for important financial decisions.

At Kwendo, I transitioned into a completely new industry (digital payroll/HR-tech) and became a subject matter expert within one month, achieving 40% market growth within six months. This demonstrates my ability to rapidly master new products and regulatory requirements while delivering results. I'm confident I can do the same with insurance products while completing my COP certification.

I understand this is a gap, and I'm asking for the opportunity to prove that my sales excellence and commitment to certification make me a strong investment. Would you be open to discussing a probationary period or conditional offer tied to certification completion?"

**Alternative if they push back**:

"I completely understand if the certification is a hard requirement that can't be waived. In that case, I'd love to stay connected and reapply once I've completed my COP, as I'm genuinely excited about bancassurance and believe my sales track record would make me a strong contributor to your team."

### Q2: "Why should we hire you over candidates who already have their COP certification?"

**Why they ask**: Testing your value proposition and self-awareness

**Model Answer**:

"That's a fair question. What I offer that certified candidates may not have is a proven, documented track record of sales excellence across multiple financial services organizations. I don't just meet targets—I consistently exceed them by 20-35%. I don't just acquire customers—I retain them at 60-70% rates through exceptional relationship management. I don't just process applications—I maintain 100% compliance and documentation accuracy.

Many candidates with COP certification may have the technical knowledge but lack the sales execution skills that drive results. I bring both: the sales excellence you need immediately, plus the commitment to gain the technical certification quickly. I'm also a proven quick learner—I've successfully transitioned across multiple financial services sectors (microfinance, fintech, HR-tech) and become effective within weeks.

Additionally, my experience training staff on products and procedures means I can contribute to your branch staff training requirements from day one. I see the COP as the final piece that completes an already strong foundation, rather than the foundation itself."

## Technical/Role-Specific Questions

### Q3: "What do you know about bancassurance, and why are you interested in this field?"

**Why they ask**: Testing industry knowledge and genuine interest

**Key points to cover**:
- Understanding of bancassurance model
- Why it's effective (customer trust, convenience, cross-selling)
- Your interest in insurance sector

**Model Answer**:

"Bancassurance is the distribution of insurance products through banking channels, leveraging the bank's existing customer relationships, branch network, and trust to sell insurance products. It's effective because customers already trust their bank for financial advice, making them more receptive to insurance products when presented by someone they know in a familiar environment.

I'm interested in bancassurance because it combines my strengths in financial services sales with the opportunity to expand into insurance—a sector with significant growth potential in Kenya as financial inclusion increases. What excites me is the consultative nature of the role: understanding customers' financial situations holistically and recommending insurance solutions that genuinely protect their assets and families. It's relationship-based selling, which is where I excel, as evidenced by my 60-70% repeat business rates.

I also see bancassurance as the future of insurance distribution in Kenya, particularly for reaching underserved markets, which aligns with my broader career mission of driving financial inclusion and empowerment."

### Q4: "How would you approach selling an insurance product to a customer who came in for a different banking service?"

**Why they ask**: Testing consultative selling skills and cross-selling ability

**Your evidence**: Cross-selling experience at Ngao, My-Credit, Schmsoft

**Model Answer**:

"I'd use a consultative, needs-based approach rather than a pushy sales pitch. First, I'd focus on delivering excellent service for their primary need—whether it's a loan, account opening, or transaction—building rapport and trust. During the conversation, I'd ask open-ended questions to understand their broader financial situation: 'What are your main financial priorities right now?' or 'What keeps you up at night financially?'

Based on their responses, I'd identify natural insurance needs. For example, if they're taking a loan for a vehicle, I'd discuss motor insurance not as an add-on but as protection for their investment. If they mention family responsibilities, I'd introduce life insurance as a way to ensure their family's security.

The key is positioning insurance as a solution to their stated needs, not as a product I'm trying to sell. I'd explain benefits in simple terms, be transparent about costs, and never pressure. At Ngao Credit, this consultative approach helped me achieve a 45% conversion rate and 60% repeat business—customers came back because they trusted I had their best interests in mind.

I'd also ensure I document their interest in our CRM for follow-up, even if they don't buy immediately, maintaining the relationship for future opportunities."

### Q5: "This role requires achieving specific sales targets for both retail and insurance products. How do you manage multiple targets simultaneously?"

**Why they ask**: Testing organizational skills and target management

**Your evidence**: Multiple product sales at My-Credit, Kwendo

**Model Answer**:

"I approach multiple targets systematically using a balanced portfolio strategy. First, I break down monthly targets into weekly and daily goals for each product category, making them manageable and trackable. I monitor my performance daily using CRM tools and adjust my activities if I'm falling behind in any category.

Second, I prioritize based on customer needs rather than just my targets. If a customer genuinely needs life insurance more than general insurance, I focus there—this builds trust and often leads to cross-selling opportunities later. However, I'm strategic about identifying which customers are good prospects for which products based on their profiles.

Third, I use cross-selling opportunities intelligently. At My-Credit, I sold multiple products (individual loans, group loans, savings) by understanding that customers who trust you for one product are more likely to buy others. I'd apply the same approach here—a customer buying motor insurance might also need life insurance, and vice versa.

At Ngao, this balanced approach helped me consistently exceed targets by 20-35% across all product categories. I also maintain a robust pipeline ensuring I have enough prospects for each product type, so I'm never scrambling at month-end."

### Q6: "How would you handle a customer complaint about an insurance claim that was denied?"

**Why they ask**: Testing customer service skills and complaint resolution

**Your evidence**: Owned customer queries at Ngao; resolved 95% within 24 hours

**Model Answer**:

"I'd handle it with empathy, transparency, and a structured resolution process. First, I'd listen actively to understand their frustration without interrupting—customers need to feel heard, especially when dealing with denied claims. I'd acknowledge their disappointment: 'I understand this is frustrating, especially when you were counting on this claim.'

Second, I'd gather all the facts: review the policy terms, claim documentation, and denial reasons. I'd explain the denial reason in simple, non-technical language, referencing specific policy clauses if applicable. If the denial was due to missing documentation or procedural issues that can be corrected, I'd guide them through the remediation process.

Third, if the denial seems questionable or the customer has valid concerns, I'd escalate to the Head Office Bancassurance team as per protocol, ensuring I document everything and follow up with the customer on progress. I'd own the issue until resolution, not just pass it off.

At Ngao Credit, I resolved 95% of customer queries within 24 hours by taking ownership and following through. Even when I couldn't give customers the answer they wanted, they appreciated the transparency and effort, which often preserved the relationship. I'd apply the same approach here—even a denied claim can be a relationship-building opportunity if handled with professionalism and empathy."

### Q7: "The role requires conducting branch staff training on bancassurance products. What's your approach to training?"

**Why they ask**: Testing training and communication skills

**Your evidence**: Trained 10+ staff at My-Credit; trained staff at Schmsoft

**Model Answer**:

"My training approach focuses on practical application rather than just theory. First, I assess the current knowledge level of the staff to tailor the training appropriately—no point teaching basics to experienced staff or assuming beginners understand advanced concepts.

Second, I use a structured format: product features and benefits, customer needs each product addresses, common objections and how to handle them, and documentation requirements. I make it interactive with role-playing scenarios where staff practice selling to 'customers' (other trainees), which builds confidence.

Third, I provide job aids—quick reference sheets with key product information, pricing, and FAQs that staff can use during customer interactions. At My-Credit, I created simple one-pagers that staff kept at their desks, which significantly improved their ability to cross-sell.

Fourth, I follow up after training to see how staff are applying what they learned, providing coaching and answering questions. Training isn't a one-time event but an ongoing process.

At My-Credit, my training program improved team capability by 40%, measured by increased cross-selling rates and reduced errors in applications. I'd bring the same practical, results-focused approach to training branch staff on bancassurance products."

### Q10: "Tell me about a time when you had to handle a difficult customer complaint. How did you resolve it?"

Not only did he not leave a negative review, but he became one of my strongest advocates, referring 5 additional clients over the next year. For the training program, I delegated some content development to experienced team members while I focused on delivery. That month, I still achieved 110% of my sales target. **Address the Elephant Early**: If they don't bring up the certification gap in the first few minutes, you might consider addressing it proactively: "Before we go further, I want to address the COP requirement directly..." This shows confidence and transparency.
